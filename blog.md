# [The Press Record Project](#the-press-record-project)

*7 November 2025* 

The Praxis Studio recently launched the [Press Record project](https://uvicpraxis.github.io/pressrecord.html), which aims to a) train and support researchers in composing and producing critical video essays about video games, b) collaborate with game studios and developers across Canada to help preserve indie games and render them accessible for post-secondary research, teaching, and learning, c) develop a reciprocal workflow for essayists that articulates game preservation and gameplay documentation with videographic criticism to support the longevity of games media and indie games in Canada, d) build and maintain a collection of gameplay footage with descriptive metadata for classroom use, e) publish our methods, findings, and video essays in Media Studies venues, and f) spark and foster an inclusive, positive-force Game Studies culture at UVic. Drop us a line if you'd like to collaborate!

# [Teaching Video Games in Catastrophic Times](#teaching-video-games-in-catastrophic-times)

*10 October 2025* 

Earlier this week, I visited UBC's Green College to speak on a panel with Sarah Christina Ganzon and Chris Patterson about ["Teaching Video Games in Catastrophic Times"](https://greencollege.ubc.ca/events/teaching-video-games-catastrophic-times-perils-pleasures-and-possibilities): "Though video games are played widely across North America, they rarely register as an influential cultural media that comments upon and critiques our political and social worlds. Despite their ability to connect players globally, video games are rarely seen as opportunities of empowerment, solidarity, organizing, and community building—but are rather understood as frivolous objects inciting moral panic. Why play, teach, or study games, when the world is on fire?"

Here are my [slides](archive/talks/ubc25/). It was an incredible event with a lively Q&A, and I want to thank Chris and UBC's Pop Culture Cluster, including Biz Nijdam, for inviting and hosting me.

# [CFP: Video Essays about Video Games](#cfp-video-essays-about-video-games)

The Praxis Studio invites submissions for a special issue of *The Digital Review* on ["Video Essays about Video Games."](https://thedigitalreview.com/submissions.html) The video essays published in this issue should contain gameplay footage and be at least 12 minutes long, captioned, and accompanied by a script or transcript. # [Degrowing Digital Projects at TAMU](#degrowing-digital-projects-at-tamu)

*18 November 2023* 

I just wrapped up a brief visit to Texas A&M's [Center of Digital Humanities Research](https://liberalarts.tamu.edu/codhr/) (CoDHR), where I met with faculty, students, and staff to discuss various topics, including games, pedagogy, infrastructure, data visualization, and minimal computing. I also gave a talk as part of their [Digital Humanities Speaker Series](https://liberalarts.tamu.edu/codhr/programs/speaker-series/). I titled it, ["Degrowing Digital Projects."](archive/talks/tamu/) CoDHR made a neat [poster](archive/talks/tamu/degrowing.png) for the event, and here's my abstract: 

"While digital projects in the humanities are frequently associated with innovation, less attention may be paid to their care and maintenance over time. This talk draws from recent research in activity theory and minimal computing as well as allied fields, such as game studies, to address the 'degrowth' of digital projects from the labor perspective. First, I outline the motives and trajectories of this perspective, and then I communicate the importance of value-sensitive design to determining three things: 1) a project’s needs and desires, 2) what work is required and from whom, and 3) how to sustain the labor involved. Degrowth aims to reduce computation's alienating effects in the interests of social organization and collective expertise, and it does so by foregrounding the cultures and habits of digital projects over their technical particulars. These issues matter for the livelihood of practitioners as gig economics and precarious labor become the norm in the technology sector."

Many thanks to Maura Ives, Bryan Tarpley, Kayley Hart, and everyone in Texas A&M's digital humanities working group for hosting me. It was a fabulous time.

# [The Pedagogy of Paper Computers at UVic](#the-pedagogy-of-paper-computers-at-uvic)

*22 May 2019*

This week, I gave a [Connect U 2019](https://web.archive.org/web/20191125201038/https://www.uvic.ca/connectu/program/index.php) talk at UVic on the pedagogy of ["Paper Computers,"](/papercomputers/) a graduate seminar on tabletop game design I taught last fall. Here are my [slides](archive/talks/connectu/). They include a rationale for the course as well as some photographs of games that students prototyped. During the talk, I explained how and why "Paper Computers" encouraged students to develop procedural literacy, experiment with low-tech methods, engage histories and cultures of media, understand contexts of play and use, design critically, and connect with communities of practice. # [Low-Tech Approaches to Digital Research for the NEH](#low-tech-approaches-to-digital-research-for-the-neh)

*26 February 2019* 

Today, I gave a talk during an NEH ODH Institute on "Textual Data and Digital Texts in the Undergraduate Classroom," directed by Lauren Coats and Emily McGinn. I titled it, ["Low-Tech Approaches to Digital Research,"](archive/talks/neh) with a focus on two questions: how to approach digital research in the classroom without assuming technical competencies in computing, and how to teach important techniques often automated or masked by tools and software. I responded to both by providing examples of low-tech prototyping as inquiry. They included the use of Markdown for web development, graphical overlays for data visualization, paper manuals for game design, cardboard models for media history, wireframes for conjectural criticism, remediations for archival work, and zines for science and technology studies. Thanks to Lauren and Emily for inviting me to participate in this timely institute, which resulted in a compelling collection of open access materials: [*Digital Texts and Textual Data: A Pedagogical Anthology*](https://hcommons.org/deposits/item/hc:28413/).

# [Tabletop Inquiry](#tabletop-inquiry)

*5 December 2018*

I wrote a reflection on my ["Paper Computers"](/papercomputers/) seminar. It's intended for graduate students in the course; however, I hope it's of use to other people, including those who are interested in media studies. It's titled ["Tabletop Inquiry"](/tabletop/) and consists of three point-form lists. # [Starting in the Middle at U. of Puget Sound](#starting-in-the-middle-at-u-of-puget-sound)

*11 May 2018* 

Yesterday, I gave a talk at the University of Puget Sound titled, "Starting in the Middle," where I outlined eight pedagogical approaches to critical media practice: 1) negotiating We/I/Them/It with Text/Context, 2) the participatory observer (who is not "detached"), 3) boundary-making in design (contrasted with appeals to abstract functions and ideal features), 4) matters of contingency and interface (as distinct from evidence or proof), 5) the entanglements of language with materials, 6) iteration as inquiry (working with machines, not delegating tasks to them), 7) multimodal composition (rather than digitization as re-presentation), and 8) accounting for shadow pasts and futures (not only certainties). Thanks to Jane Carlin and Collins Memorial Library for inviting me, and to Jamie L. Spaine for coordinating my visit. It was exciting to see what Puget Sound is doing with media and [their new makerspace](https://library.pugetsound.edu/makerspace).

# [Prototyping as Composition at Syracuse](#prototyping-as-composition-at-syracuse)

*7 March 2016*

Here's my abstract for the "Prototyping as Composition" talk: 

"Across the humanities, art, design, engineering, as well as numerous memory institutions, researchers are giving digital fabrication techniques such as 3-D printing significant attention. In response, this talk examines the affordances of fabrication for scholarly communication, with particular attention to rapid prototyping, or the iterative production of abstract models in tactile form, as a compositional strategy. Instead of suggesting that writing is somehow secondary to the primary experience of making things, I underscore how prototyping is intricately entangled with archival research and the writing that emerges through that research."

# [Popup Makerspace in Lima at HASTAC 2014](#popup-makerspace-in-lima-at-hastac-2014)

*12 September 2014* 

Back in April, Nina Belojevic, Shaun Macpherson, Kaitlynn McQueston, and I traveled to the Ministry of Culture in Lima, where we set up a popup makerspace for [HASTAC 2014](https://web.archive.org/web/20140517121345/http://hastac2014.org/). Nina [describes](https://maker.uvic.ca/lima2/) the makerspace's theme like so: "The admittedly experimental theme of the popup makerspace was, 'Whose Hand Am I Holding Anyway?' We used the space's temporary infrastructure to draw public attention to the intricate relationships between bodies, data, immediacy, and distance in a 'global' digital economy. We first cast hands in plaster and then---with anyone who stumbled upon the space---created silicon proxies of those hands. The proxies were embedded with sensors, and later in the conference we wrote an Arduino sketch to not only log when the sensors were triggered but also display messages via a serial readout. Conference participants could shake hands with these rather defamiliarizing proxies, an often amusing encounter that was immediately confirmed on a screen in the space. The handshake data was then logged so that we'd know, by the conference's conclusion, how many people the proxies had 'met.'"

# [CFI/FCI Grant for a Humanities Makerspace](#cfifci-grant-for-a-humanities-makerspace)

*11 March 2014*

I'm very happy to announce that the [Maker Lab](https://maker.uvic.ca/cfi/) received support from the Canada Foundation for Innovation (CFI) / Fondation canadienne pour l’innovation (FCI) John R.
